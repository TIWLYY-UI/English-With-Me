Foundation

Parts of Speech

Every English word belongs to one of eight categories. Understanding them is the first step.

A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea.

Types: Proper, Common, Abstract, Collective, Countable, Uncountable

  • Bangkok is a beautiful city. (proper)
  • She has a dog. (common)
  • Freedom is priceless. (abstract)
  • A flock of birds flew past. (collective)
A verb expresses an action, occurrence, or state of being.

Types: Action, Linking, Auxiliary, Modal

  • She runs every morning. (action)
  • He is a teacher. (linking)
  • They have finished. (auxiliary)
  • You should study hard. (modal)
Adjectives describe or modify nouns and pronouns.
  • She wore a beautiful dress.
  • The old man smiled.
  • It was a sunny day.

Order: opinion → size → age → shape → colour → origin → material → purpose + noun

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Many end in -ly.
  • She sings beautifully. (how)
  • He arrived early. (when)
  • They sat nearby. (where)
  • It is very hot today. (degree)
Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition.
Subject:
I, you, he, she, it, we, they
Object:
me, you, him, her, it, us, them
Possessive:
my, your, his, her, our, their
Reflexive:
myself, yourself, himself…
Prepositions show relationships between words in a sentence.
  • The book is on the table. (place)
  • She arrived at 9 am. (time)
  • He walked through the park. (movement)
Conjunctions join words, phrases, or clauses.
  • I like tea and coffee. (coordinating)
  • Although it rained, we went out. (subordinating)
  • I was tired; however, I kept going. (correlative)
Interjections express emotion and are often followed by an exclamation mark.
  • Wow! That's amazing!
  • Ouch! That hurt.
  • Hmm, let me think about it.
Structure

Sentence Structure

A well-formed sentence needs a subject and a verb at minimum.

S + V
Type 1

Simple Sentence

One independent clause with a subject and verb.

Subject + Verb (+ Object)

"She reads." / "Tom loves pizza."

S+V , S+V
Type 2

Compound Sentence

Two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction.

Clause + and/but/so/or + Clause

"I was tired, but I kept going."

Sub + Main
Type 3

Complex Sentence

One independent + one dependent clause with a subordinating conjunction.

Because / Although / When + clause

"Although it rained, we went out."

Articles

Articles: a, an, the

Knowing when to use articles correctly is one of the most important grammar skills.

📌 Indefinite Articles — a / an

Use a before consonant sounds, an before vowel sounds.
  • She is a teacher.
  • He ate an apple.
  • It was a unique idea. (unique = /juːnɪk/ consonant sound)
  • Wait an hour. (hour = /aʊər/ vowel sound)

📌 Definite Article — the

Use the when the noun is specific or already known to the listener.
  • Close the door. (specific door)
  • The sun rises in the east. (unique)
  • I saw a dog. The dog was huge. (mentioned before)
  • She plays the piano. (instruments)
Voice

Active & Passive Voice

Change the focus of a sentence by switching between active and passive constructions.

✅ Active Voice

Subject + Verb + Object

The subject performs the action. Direct, clear, and concise.

  • Tom wrote the letter.
  • She is eating lunch.
  • The chef cooked the meal.

🔄 Passive Voice

Object + be + Past Participle + (by + Subject)

The subject receives the action. Used when the doer is unknown or unimportant.

  • The letter was written by Tom.
  • Lunch is being eaten.
  • The meal was cooked by the chef.
Conditionals

Conditional Sentences

Four types of conditionals to express real and hypothetical situations.

0

Zero Conditional — General Truths

If + Present Simple , Present Simple

"If you heat water to 100°C, it boils."

1

First Conditional — Real & Possible

If + Present Simple , will + Base Verb

"If it rains, I will stay home."

2

Second Conditional — Unreal / Unlikely

If + Past Simple , would + Base Verb

"If I were rich, I would travel the world."

3

Third Conditional — Impossible / Past Regret

If + Past Perfect , would have + Past Participle

"If I had studied harder, I would have passed."

Vocabulary

Essential Vocabulary

Key words every English learner should know, with definitions and examples.

🏃
Achieve
verb
To successfully reach a goal or result.
"She achieved excellent grades."
✨
Brilliant
adjective
Exceptionally clever or talented.
"He is a brilliant student."
🌟
Courage
noun
The ability to do something that frightens one.
"It takes courage to speak in public."
📚
Discover
verb
To find something for the first time.
"They discovered a new species."
💪
Efficient
adjective
Achieving maximum productivity with minimum effort.
"She is an efficient worker."
⚡
Frequently
adverb
Often; at short intervals.
"He frequently visits the library."
🌱
Improve
verb
To make or become better.
"Practice will improve your English."
🔑
Significant
adjective
Important; of great consequence.
"This is a significant discovery."
🎯
Opportunity
noun
A set of circumstances that makes something possible.
"Seize every opportunity to learn."
